Planets are born out of clouds of dust along with the stars they orbit on timescales of millions of years. Dr. Kuznetsova searches for planetary ingredients in the disks of accreting material around young stars.

An example of one of these planet forming regions is the hourglass nebula, captured here by Webb. We view this planet forming region edge on with dust being ejected above and below the rotating system.

Dr. Cara Battersby uses our own milky way as a laboratory to understand distant galaxies. She uses observations of our galaxy but also simulations of the turbulent environment around the center of the milky way where stars form.
We can take a look at one of these simulations. This is a model of how particles in gas interact at the turbulent environment of the center of a milkyway like galaxy. The simulation take in to acocunt information about the magnetic field, temperature, density, and lots of other factors. Dr. Battersby's group uses simulations like these to learn about the gas dynamics and star formation histories of other galaxies as well as out own!

Dr. Trump works on a tiny field of view, size of 10 thicknesses of your fingernail just above the big dipper called CEERS. It was one of the first things observed by JWST, before the classic images of stephan's quintet and the carina nebula.

We can zoom in on the image to see just how many galaxies there are (ceers_crop.png if you want to do it yourself with the proper projection)
We can calcualte the line-of sight distances of these galaxies and create fly-throughs of CEERS. This is a flight to Masie's galaxy at z = 11, compared to z~1.4 from the SDSS image we saw.

All the bright things are galaxies. So are the dim things! a suprising result has been the little red dots that pepper the observations. They are compact and red! Maybe early actreting supermassive black holes.

Dr. Trump's group is most intersted in super distant black holes that occupy the galaxies in these surveys. Once thought to be mathematical curiosities, black holes are now known to be present in the center of every galaxy. The group seeks to understand the birth and growth of astrophysical black holes, using multi-wavelength observations to map small-scale environment, measuring black hole mass, spin, accretion flows, and winds. It also extends to the large-scale interplay between black holes and their host galaxies, particularly understanding how the first black hole “seeds” collapse within the first galaxies at cosmic dawn.
